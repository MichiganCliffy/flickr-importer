April 19th: I'm in my car, leaving from Tahoe, destination - somewhere in Utah (I haven't made up my mind yet). It's a long drive, with a minor stop in Nevada due to a speeding ticket (damn). It takes about 12 hours to get to Salt Lake City, from there I head south and finally call it quits at a Holiday Inn somewhere off I15. I was pleasantly surprised at the scenery in Eastern Nevada - I had no idea there were mountains in Nevada, I always assumed it was mostly desert. Western Utah is pretty dull - all desert and salt flats.

April 20th: I make it to Bryce Canyon. It is still winter, and at an elevation of 10k ft, there is still snow on the ground. The contrast between the red rocks and mud, the white of the snow, and the blue, blue sky is absolutely fantastic. I get a campsite at the park, and set out for a hike.

This park is amazing. The colors, rock formations, and big sky landscapes are breath taking. I highly recommend a trip to Bryce, it is truly a unique place. I only spent one night there (one cold night). I decide to backtrack a bit and head west to Zion.

April 21st: I arrive at Zion National Park. The drive into the park is awesome - complete with a tunnel drive though the rock canyon. The park reminds me of Yosemite Valley, but instead of gray rock, there is red rock.

I get a campsite for a night, and decide to warm up my mountain bike. I pedal around the park, stopping and locking up the bike at various points to hike around and take pictures. I managed to loose myself a bit, taking pictures and wandering, emerging from some back road trail I came across a fellow lost traveller. Turns out she is a writer/photographer for a small Los Angeles magazine - she takes my picture (she thinks I look like some sort of back country explorer). I head back to the car and cook up some serious grub.

It starts to rain a bit, and I start planning my route to Moab. I decide to drive back past Bryce and through Grand Escalante National Forrest.

April 22nd: I start my drive. What a wild ride?!? I pass through a snow storm, pass by monstrous mesa's, through a wind storm, cut through Capitol Reef National Park, hit a sand storm, and finally arrive in Moab. I decide to treat myself to a night in a real bed and get a hotel room in town. I so needed that shower.

Moab is a little town in the south east corner of Utah. There is no real industry there, other than adventure tourism and some random drilling and grazing sites. The city is surrounded by Arches and Canyonlands National Parts, and the Colorado River runs nearby.

The area surrounding Moab is known for spectacular mountain biking, hiking/canyoning, rafting, and rock climbing. I would also add photography, the landscape is gorgeous, with dramatic big-sky vistas that are simply stunning. This is a part of the US that I will have to visit again.

April 23rd - April 26th: My days are made up of hiking, biking, and taking pictures. I generally alternated between hiking and biking, mostly to change up muscle usage. I biked the Slickrock trail, as well as a couple of warm up rides at Bartlett Wash and Klondike Bluffs, and a killer ride called the Flat Pass Trail.

Slickrock (basically sandstone) is a lot of fun to ride on. Bike tires tend to stick to the rock, allowing the rider to climb and descend some very steep inclines. The Klondike Bluffs trail had some petrified dinosaurs footprints along the trail, not your usual scenery. The Flat Pass Trail was a beast, 19 miles round trip, 4 stream crossings, multiple shelf climbs, and some wicked descents. So much fun.

On my off days, I hiked around Arches National Park, as well as a drive and hike at Dead Horse Point.

April 27th: The long drive home (and I mean long). I decide to drive back using Highway 50, "America's Loneliest Highway." It lived up to it's name, and was a very interesting drive. There really is not much in Nevada, nothing at all. I got to Reno, and decided to push on through home to San Francisco. By the time I got home, my right butt cheek was cramping up (I really missed Cruise Control on that drive).
